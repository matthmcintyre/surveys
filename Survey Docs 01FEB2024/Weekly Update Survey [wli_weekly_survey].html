16.

So how much physical activity should you aim for? Well, that depends on your weight loss goal and how active you currently are. The American College of Sports Medicine recommends 150-250 minutes per week of moderate-intensity physical activity for modest weight loss. Greater amounts (i.e. greater than 250 minutes per week) provide clinically significant weight loss.


{CONTINUE BUTTON}

    [CONCEPT: wli_week_1_exercise_1]


17.

Let's break that down:

  • Aerobic activity. Aim for at least 150 minutes a week (e.g., 30 minutes, 5 times a week) of moderate aerobic activity, or 75 minutes a week of vigorous aerobic activity. Moderate activity includes brisk walking, skating, volleyball, or mowing the lawn. You'll know you're in the "moderate intensity" range if you can talk but not sing while you're active. Vigorous activity includes running and aerobic dancing — you're in the "vigorous" activity range if you have to stop for a breath every few words.
  • You can also do a combination of moderate and vigorous activity. And it's best to spread this activity out over the course of the week, not cram it all into one trip to the gym.
  • Strength training. Aim to strength train at least twice a week. Consider free weights, weight machines or activities that use your own body weight — like rock climbing or heavy gardening.


{CONTINUE BUTTON}

    [CONCEPT: wli_week_1_exercise_2]


18.

If you're not used to doing this much physical activity, it may seem like a lot! It's okay to work towards these goals by starting small (e.g., with 10 minutes per day or less of light-intensity aerobic activity), and increasing the duration and intensity as you get stronger.

Stay hydrated & control cravings. Drink plenty of water throughout the day, especially after physical activity. Research shows that drinking water before meals can also reduce appetite and help with weight loss.

19.

How should I decide which kinds of physical activity to try?


Would you rather exercise solo or in a group? Group exercise is a good fit if you are outgoing or looking to meet new people, if you find solo exercise boring, or if you have a hard time staying motivated when you exercise alone. Solo exercise is a good match if you want to work out at home or on your own schedule. Try out different ways of getting active and see what fits your style — the best exercise is the one you'll do!

Choose activities that accommodate your health issues. If you have a history of athletic injuries or existing health problems, you may want to talk to your doctor before starting a new exercise routine. Most medical conditions won't rule out exercise completely, but certain types of exercise may be a better fit for you.

    Lie with your back on the floor and knees bent at 90 degrees. Keeping your feet flat on the floor, lift your hips upwards by pushing against the floor with your heels. The final position should result in a straight line (aka a 'bridge') from the back of your shoulders to your knees.

    40.

    This is a great core exercise: the forearm plank.

    To do it, forearms are placed on the ground with elbows directly below the shoulders so that the forearms are parallel to the body and about a shoulders-width apart. Then a straight line can be imagined from the shoulders down to the heels. A successful plank is maintaining that straight line relationship from shoulders, to hips, to heels.

    Adjusting foot positioning may be necessary to help maintain that straight line position. For a variation of this exercise, knees can be placed on the ground. A typical routine (2-3 times per week) involves holding the plank position for 30 seconds at a time, working up to 1 minute as your core gets stronger.

    43.

    Let's talk about how hunger works. Your body uses two hormones called ghrelin and leptin to control hunger. Ghrelin is known as "the hunger hormone" because it makes you want to eat, and leptin is "the satiety hormone" because it tells you to stop eating as you're getting full.

    The levels of leptin and ghrelin in your body depend on things you'd expect, like the time since your last meal, what kind of food you ate, and how active you've been. But it turns out those hormone levels also depend on other factors, like how much sleep you got last night, and how much stress you're feeling.


    {CONTINUE BUTTON}

        [CONCEPT: wli_week_9_content_expectations_1]


    44.

    When you don't get enough sleep, ghrelin levels are high, which makes you feel more hungry. And even after eating a healthy meal, your leptin levels stay low — meaning you don't feel satisfied, and you might still be hungry when you shouldn't be.

    Stress can have similar effects on hunger hormones. One study found that women who were under social stress had higher ghrelin levels and lower leptin levels than women who were under less stress. In other words, their bodies were sending more "I'm hungry" signals and fewer "I'm full" signals.

    What does this mean? It means that keeping up with a healthy diet isn't just about willpower, it's also about taking good care of yourself. It means that getting enough sleep and keeping your stress levels low are important because you want your body to work with you — not against you — as you try to lose weight. More sleep and less stress means that you're likely to be less hungry, less often.

    49.

    There's a lot of buzz about the role of genetics in weight loss, but scientific research in this field is still in very early stages.

    What does that mean? Well, most studies have looked for genetic associations with weight or body mass index (BMI), but not with weight loss specifically. And the genetic variants associated with weight might be different from those associated with weight loss.

    Other studies have examined a small number of genetic variants when exploring the complicated relationships between diet, exercise, and weight. Often times, their findings aren't confirmed by later studies. But something as complex as weight loss is likely to depend on many different genetic factors, not just a handful.


    {CONTINUE BUTTON}

        [CONCEPT: wli_week_11_content_expectations_1]


    50.

    So, how can we discover genetic variants associated with weight loss in this study? By using a much more powerful scientific approach, called 'GWAS', which stands for genome-wide association study.

    A GWAS measures hundreds of thousands of genetic variants to find out which ones are most strongly associated with weight loss. That's a big improvement over looking at just a few genetic variants!

    51.

    Why hasn't anyone performed a GWAS of weight loss before? Usually, a study needs to have a lot of participants in order to uncover genetic variants using GWAS. And so far, studies about weight loss and genetics have been too small.

    At 23andMe, we're excited to be leading this breakthrough weight loss intervention study, using GWAS to discover genetic associations with weight loss. And we're happy that you're part of it!
